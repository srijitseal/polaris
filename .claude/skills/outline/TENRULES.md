# Ten Simple Rules for Structuring Papers - Actionable Reference

Distilled from Mensh & Kording (2017) for practical outlining.

---

## Principles (Rules 1-4)

### Rule 1: One Central Contribution

**Summary:** Focus the entire paper on a single main message, communicated in the title.

**Violation signals:**

- Title doesn't capture the main finding
- Paper tries to claim multiple unrelated contributions
- Reader can't summarize the paper in one sentence after reading

**Applies to:** Title, every section, every paragraph

**Action:** Before outlining, write a single sentence stating the central contribution. Return to it constantly.

---

### Rule 2: Write for Non-Experts

**Summary:** You are the least qualified person to judge clarity. Define terms, avoid jargon, minimize cognitive load.

**Violation signals:**

- Undefined acronyms or technical terms
- Assumes reader knows your specific methods
- Too many concepts introduced at once

**Applies to:** All sections, especially Abstract and Introduction

**Action:** For each sentence, ask: "Would someone outside my subfield understand this?"

---

### Rule 3: Context-Content-Conclusion (C-C-C)

**Summary:** Every unit (paper, section, paragraph) has beginning (context), middle (content), and end (conclusion).

**Violation signals:**

- Reader asks "why was I told that?" (missing context)
- Reader asks "so what?" (missing conclusion)
- Content appears before reader is prepared for it

**Applies to:**

- **Paper scale:** Intro (context) -> Results (content) -> Discussion (conclusion)
- **Paragraph scale:** First sentence (context) -> Body (content) -> Last sentence (conclusion)

**Action:** For each paragraph in your outline, annotate: `[C]` context, `[Co]` content, `[Cn]` conclusion

---

### Rule 4: Avoid Zig-Zag, Use Parallelism

**Summary:** Each topic should appear in only one place. Related ideas should use parallel structure.

**Violation signals:**

- Same topic discussed in multiple non-adjacent sections
- Jumping between unrelated subjects
- Similar points presented with different sentence structures

**Applies to:** Paragraph ordering, sentence structure

**Action:**

- List all topics; ensure each appears in one location only
- When presenting multiple similar items (e.g., three reasons), use identical syntax

---

## Components (Rules 5-8)

### Rule 5: Abstract = Complete Story

**Summary:** The abstract must convey the entire message. Most readers read only this.

**Structure:**

1. **Context** (1-2 sentences): Broader field -> specific gap
2. **Content** (2-3 sentences): "Here we..." + key results with numbers
3. **Conclusion** (1 sentence): Broader significance

**Violation signals:**

- Results mentioned before gap is established
- No specific numbers or findings
- Missing broader significance at end
- Gap and results don't fit together like lock and key

---

### Rule 6: Introduction = Why It Matters

**Summary:** Establish the gap through progressive narrowing, ending with "Here we..."

**Structure:**

1. **Paragraph 1:** Field gap (broad context)
2. **Paragraph 2:** Subfield gap (narrowing)
3. **Paragraph 3:** Paper gap (specific untested hypothesis)
4. **Paragraph 4:** "Here, we..." summary (no detailed results)

**Violation signals:**

- Broad literature review without gap focus
- Results appear in introduction
- Reader can't identify the specific gap being filled
- Missing "Here we..." transition

---

### Rule 7: Results = Declarative Statements + Figures

**Summary:** Results are a sequence of logical statements supported by data, not a chronological narrative.

**Structure:**

- **Subsection titles:** Declarative statements (conclusions)
- **First paragraph:** Overall approach and key methods
- **Each paragraph:** Question -> Data -> Answer
- **Figures:** Title states conclusion; legend explains how

**Violation signals:**

- Subsection titles are methods, not findings
- Chronological narrative of experiments
- Data presented without interpretation
- Figure titles don't state conclusions

---

### Rule 8: Discussion = Gap Filled + Limitations + Future

**Summary:** Explain how results filled the gap, acknowledge limitations, describe broader impact.

**Structure:**

1. **Paragraph 1:** Summary of findings (gist of Results)
2. **Paragraphs 2-N:** Limitations (weakness -> context -> future)
3. **Final paragraph:** Broader significance, future directions

**Violation signals:**

- Repeats Results instead of interpreting them
- Limitations presented without context or mitigation
- Doesn't connect back to Introduction gap
- Missing broader significance

---

## Process (Rules 9-10)

### Rule 9: Invest in Title, Abstract, Figures, Outline

**Summary:** Allocate time proportional to impact. Most readers see only title and abstract.

**Priority order:**

1. Title (most read)
2. Abstract (second most read)
3. Figures and their titles
4. Results subsection headers
5. Paragraph first sentences
6. Everything else

**Action:** Write one sentence per planned paragraph. Ensure each has a defined role in the story.

---

### Rule 10: Iterate with Feedback

**Summary:** Writing is optimization. Get external input. Be willing to trash and rewrite.

**Warning signs that need revision:**

- Can't explain outline to colleague in few minutes
- Non-specific feedback from reviewers (didn't "get" the story)
- Very specific feedback (logic within paragraph insufficient)

**Action:** After outlining, read only first sentences of each paragraph. Does it tell the complete story?
